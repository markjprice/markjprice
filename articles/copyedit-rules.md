# Chapter 1 Copyedit Rules Summary

This review followed standard US English copyediting practice with emphasis on:

- Prefering clear, direct sentences over inflated or repetitive phrasing.
- Fixing grammar, agreement, tense consistency, and faulty parallel structure.
- Correcting typos, spacing issues, missing words, and broken compounds.
- Standardizing terminology and capitalization for product names and headings.
- Improving punctuation around parentheticals, lists, and introductory elements.
- Removing ambiguity when a sentence could mislead a beginner.
- Tightening wordy construction without changing meaning.
- Preserving the authorial voice where possible while improving readability.
- Favoring idiomatic US English usage.
- Watching for consistency in hyphenation, quotation marks, and technical style.

## Recurring issues found in this chapter

- Missing or incorrect subject-verb agreement.
- Broken or missing spaces inside compound terms.
- Nonparallel list items.
- Overloaded sentences that try to do too much at once.
- Headings that read like notes rather than polished book prose.
- Repetition of filler phrases such as *in this section*, *let’s now*, and *you can read more at the following link*.
- AI-product descriptions that drift into dated or promotional wording.
- Places where the prose sounds generated because it uses generic abstractions instead of concrete meaning.

## Good habits to apply in later chapters

- Keep chapter overview entries grammatically parallel.
- Use one sentence for one main idea whenever possible.
- Prefer concrete verbs over abstract nouns.
- Treat examples, instructions, and recommendations as separate units.
- Cut stock intensifiers such as *very*, *significantly*, *powerful*, and *important* unless they add real meaning.
- Replace vague praise with specific capability or limitation.
- Watch headings and figure/table references for numbering consistency.
- Read aloud to catch phrasing that sounds synthetic or overpolished.
