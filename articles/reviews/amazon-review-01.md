# Not for someone looking for a concise education in current C#

*Reviewed in the United States on February 3, 2026* Format: Paperback, Verified Purchase

The author is a C# expert and historian and the book would be good casual reading for like-minded people. It is a poor option for someone interested in a concise explanation of development in current C# and .NET. Note that most glowing reviews are from experienced C# developers. I have taught university-level programming intro courses and would not recommend this book to a student.

AI noted described it thusly: "It tries to be a history lesson, a beginner’s guide, and an advanced reference all at once, and the result is … bloated, nonlinear, and constantly interrupting itself with version‑to‑version commentary."

Instead of building up concepts straightforwardly, the author ping-pongs between basic and advanced concepts in a jarring manner. For instance, in Chapter 2, shortly after introducing basic concepts such as whitespace, he goes into an unnecessary multi-page digression about hidden, system-generated namespace files. Then he has the reader write a program to count methods in assemblies (before even explaining what a method is). Interesting to an intermediate C# developer; baffling and frustrating to a new learner. This sort of thing happens regularly throughout the book.

The author enjoys sharing backstories about how C# and .NET have changed over the years, as this is his specialty. He goes on at length about older versions and future versions. But for most readers, this doesn't matter. People learning C# don't care about how something was done differently in C# ten years ago, nor do they care about what might change next year. People learning C# don't need to worry about 'which version of the SDK they are targeting'. That is a senior professional dev concern, so put all of that and all of the C# history in an appendix at the end. In an intro book, it's a frequent distraction.

The author will sometimes describe something at length, and then say, "but don't do it like that- no one does. I was just showing you how it used to be done back in C# version no-one-cares, so you can understand why the current way is at is".

All of this excess material results in a book of over 800 tightly-bound pages, making it physically heavy and difficult to even keep open. It could be much shorter.
