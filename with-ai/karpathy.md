**Who is Andrej Karpathy, and why does he matter?**

If you are new to modern AI, the name Andrej Karpathy may not mean much yet. But in the world of machine learning and software engineering, he is one of the most influential figures of the last decade.

# Early career and academic work

Andrej Karpathy is a Slovak-Canadian computer scientist who became widely known during the deep learning boom of the 2010s. He completed his PhD at Stanford University, where he worked on computer vision and neural networks under Fei-Fei Li, one of the leading researchers in the field.

His early work focused on teaching machines to understand images and natural language together. This included systems that could generate captions for images, which at the time felt like science fiction but is now commonplace.

# OpenAI and Tesla

Karpathy’s career is closely tied to two of the most important organizations in modern AI:

* **OpenAI**: He was one of the early researchers at OpenAI, contributing to the development of deep learning systems that would eventually lead to large language models like GPT.
* **Tesla**: He later became Director of AI at Tesla, where he led the computer vision team responsible for Autopilot. His work focused on training neural networks to interpret real-world driving environments using camera data.

At Tesla, he helped pioneer the idea of treating real-world data as the primary driver of system improvement, rather than relying heavily on simulation or handcrafted rules.

# Educator and communicator

Karpathy is also known for his ability to explain complex topics clearly. His lectures and posts have helped thousands of developers understand neural networks, backpropagation, and modern AI systems.

One of his most famous contributions is the idea that:

> “The hottest new programming language is English.”

This captures a major shift in how software is being built. Instead of writing every line of code manually, developers increasingly describe what they want in natural language and let AI systems generate the implementation.

# Why he is relevant to this book

Karpathy matters in the context of this book because he represents a turning point in programming:

* He has worked at the center of both research (OpenAI) and real-world deployment (Tesla).
* He has seen firsthand how AI changes the way engineers work.
* He is one of the first prominent engineers to openly describe programming as moving from code-first to prompt-first workflows.

When he says that programming is shifting toward natural language, he is not speculating. He is describing what he and other leading engineers are already doing.

# The bigger picture

Karpathy’s perspective highlights an uncomfortable but important truth:

* Writing code is becoming *less* important.
* Understanding systems, architecture, and behavior is becoming *more* important.

In other words, the value of a developer is shifting from *typing code* to *thinking clearly about what should be built*.

That shift is at the heart of this book.
