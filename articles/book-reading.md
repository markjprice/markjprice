# Most people barely read at all

Before you even get to “how much of a book is read,” the bigger reality is:

* Around [**40–50% of adults don’t read a single book in a year** and the **median reader finishes only 2–3 books per year**](https://yougov.co.uk/entertainment/articles/51730-40-of-britons-havent-read-a-single-book-in-the-last-12-months)
* Roughly [**half of adults don’t regularly read**](https://readingagency.org.uk/adult-reading-research-report-2024/) at all
* In the US, only [**48% finished even one book in a year**](https://bookriot.com/american-reading-habits-2022/)

# Completion rates: rare, partial, but revealing

There is no widely published “programming book completion rate,” but there are a few proxies. Kobo reported [**~62% completion for mystery novels**] and top-performing books can reach **80%+ completion**](https://johnmjennings.com/how-many-people-finish-books/), but that’s exceptional. Fiction is easier to binge. Technical books are almost certainly **much lower**.

Across publishing and edtech (no single canonical study, but consistent anecdotal evidence), many nonfiction books are **browsed, not read linearly** because a large portion of buyers only read **intro + first chapter** and then jump to **specific sections**. Many never finish the book.

This aligns with broader behavior that people read [**for specific information**](https://www.theguardian.com/books/article/2024/jul/24/more-than-a-third-of-uk-adults-have-given-up-reading-for-pleasure-study-finds), not cover-to-cover, especially in nonfiction.

# Programming books are a different beast

Programming books behave more like:

* Reference manuals
* Courses
* Problem-solving tools

From both research patterns and developer behavior:
1. **Scanning mode**: “I need to learn X quickly”
2. **Task-driven reading**: “How do I do dependency injection / async / EF Core?”
3. **Drop-off when friction appears**: setup issues, confusion, too much theory

This means completion is not the goal. Utility per minute is.

---

# Hypothetical completion rates

A plausible a mental model, especially for technical books, might look like this:
* ~20–40%: skim only
* ~30–50%: read first chapter or two
* ~10–20%: go deep into multiple chapters
* <10%: read cover-to-cover

# What this means for where you put effort

My “double effort on Chapter 1” instinct is good, but too narrow because I've been optimizing for linear readers, but most of your audience is nonlinear.

I actually have three entry points:

1. **The book opening (Chapter 1)**

   * Hooks motivation
   * Sets tone
   * Determines if they continue

2. **Random mid-book chapters (entry via search/TOC)**

   * Many readers start here
   * These must stand alone

3. **Code examples / snippets**

   * Often the *first thing* people look at

If Chapter 5 is someone’s first exposure, it must feel like a good first chapter.

---

# The “first friction point” matters more than Chapter 1

Most drop-off happens when:

* Setup fails
* Concepts get abstract
* Examples stop working

Most authors:

* Over-polish Chapter 1
* Rush Chapters 4–10

But readers:

* Often land directly in those chapters
* Judge the book from there

For my latest books, I've added a box to the start of each chapter with link to an online page that shows setup if a reader has skipped *Chapter 1*.

---

# End chapters are low ROI

Final chapters:

* Have the lowest readership
* Are often skipped entirely

Unless they’re:

* Projects
* Cheat sheets
* Summaries

---

# A better effort distribution model

- **1. Opening (Chapter 1 + intro): 2× effort**: Hook, clarity, confidence.
- **2. Early progression (Chapters 2–3): 2× effort**: This is where people quit.
- **3. Core “landing zone” chapters: 1.5× effort**: These are the most searched topics or most useful in isolation.
- **4. Late chapters: 0.5× effort**: Unless they are practical capstones.

# Practical tactics that align with real reading behavior

These matter more than polishing prose:

## Make every chapter standalone

* Brief recap of prerequisites
* Clear “what you’ll learn”
* Minimal dependency on earlier chapters

## Optimize for scanning

* Strong headings
* Code first, explanation second
* Short sections

## Reduce friction aggressively

* Pre-empt errors
* Show expected output
* Avoid “magic steps”

## Reward partial readers

Assume this might be the only chapter they read

---

# Conclusion

You are not writing a book that people will read linearly. You are writing a structured knowledge system that people will sample unevenly. Success looks like “This book solved my problem” not “I finished this book”.
